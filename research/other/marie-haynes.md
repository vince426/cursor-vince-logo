## "The purpose of Search is changing. It is shifting from a place where we go to get information to one where we have our agents get things done for us."

Dr. Marie Haynes is a former veterinarian who pivoted to SEO in 2008 and has built her practice around studying Google's algorithms in unusual depth. Her company won the Wix SEO competition in 2019, and she recently published "SEO in the Gemini Era: the story of how AI changed Google Search." She has written a newsletter on search for fourteen years and runs the Search Bar Pro paid community, where she shares prompts, strategies, and weekly calls with clients at the cutting edge of AI and search. Her approach is forensic rather than tactical — she reads trial testimony, studies API documentation, and interprets Quality Rater Guidelines as primary sources rather than summarizing what others say about them. The clients she works with are on the front edge of watching Google's systems change, and much of what she writes is drawn from the pattern that becomes visible when you see a large enough sample of what helps and what hurts across real sites.

The foundational shift Marie is describing to clients in 2026 is not incremental. Search is no longer primarily a retrieval tool. Sundar Pichai has said directly that "a lot of what are just information-seeking queries will be agentic in Search," and Google's head of search Liz Reid has said that while humans will still want to hear from other people, she believes the web will reach a state where much of it is simply agents talking to each other. On desktop, when Marie expands an AI Overview, traditional search disappears entirely and AI Mode takes over, with websites available if needed rather than central to the experience. Google has also released tools — an Alt+Space launcher for Windows that opens searches in AI Mode, a Mac equivalent that pops Gemini over whatever you are doing, and Gemini in Chrome that lets users converse across open tabs — that accelerate this shift from browsing to acting. The browser is becoming a place to do things, not just to read things.


## "User satisfaction is by far the most important thing we should be optimizing for."

Marie's most sustained argument over the past year concerns what Google's ranking systems are actually optimizing for, and why most SEO advice misses it. She pieced this together from testimony in the DOJ versus Google trial, from Liz Reid's appeal declaration, and from academic papers on the systems involved. Google's ranking process has three components. Traditional systems create an initial pool of candidates. AI systems — RankBrain, DeepRank, and especially RankEmbed BERT — re-rank the top twenty to thirty documents. Those AI systems are then continuously fine-tuned by two inputs: scores from Quality Raters who compare results from "frozen" versus "retrained" algorithms, and live user experiments where a small percentage of real searchers see old results against new ones, and their behavior shapes which version wins.

The key detail is what Google is measuring in those live tests. It is not tracking individual URL performance. It is teaching the AI to recognize patterns in what satisfying content looks like. The system learns to identify types of content that resolve user intent, then predicts whether new candidates fit that mold. Google's Glue system is a massive table of user activity — the text of every query, language, location, device, what appeared on the SERP, what was clicked, what was hovered over, and how long the session lasted. RankEmbed BERT is trained directly on that click and query data from actual users. This is what Google does not want to hand to competitors under the DOJ ruling, because it is the core of the competitive advantage. Marie's conclusion from all of this is that if you look credible to AI ranking systems but users who actually visit your page do not stay, do not scroll, and do not engage, you are not just failing to rank — you are actively training Google's systems to trust you less. She explicitly warns against optimizing for vector search or cosine similarity. The goal is to be the result that genuinely satisfies the searcher, because the systems are tuned to find exactly that.


## "Google's ranking systems are a collection of AI systems that work together with one goal in mind — to present searchers with pages they are likely to find helpful."

Marie has spent years analyzing sites that recovered from core updates and sites that did not, and her December 2025 core update case studies surface what actually moves the needle. The word "trust" appears 191 times in Google's Quality Rater Guidelines. For online stores, the raters are explicitly told that reliable customer service is vitally important. One medical eCommerce client she worked with had been suppressed after the August 2024 core update because they had significant shipping problems that damaged their reputation. Despite extensive technical and content improvements, the AI Mode reputation prompt — "Make a chart showing the perceived trust in this brand over time" — consistently showed serious concerns to anyone who ran it. Recovery only came when the business actually fixed its customer service and that improvement became visible in third-party signals. The recovery is not reversible through content alone when the underlying trust problem is real.

The pattern she saw across sites that recovered with the December 2025 update was consistent: genuine improvement in how the world perceives the business and genuine improvement in the content itself. The answer is not legacy technical fixes. Disavowing links, monitoring HTTPS implementation, or obsessing over Core Web Vitals scores does not solve a core update problem — though she notes that a visibly slow Largest Contentful Paint does frustrate users, and user frustration does feed the satisfaction signals Google measures. The answer is making the page better for the person reading it, and ensuring the brand's off-site reputation reflects the quality of what it delivers. Core update recovery, she cautions, typically takes more than a year even when everything is done right, and even then is not guaranteed because Google's algorithms continue to change.


## "What people click on in AI Overviews is content that is richer and deeper."

One of the sharpest observations in Marie's recent work concerns what AI Overviews have done to "commodity content." Commodity content is information widely available in many places on the web — the kind of page that aggregates known facts and paraphrases from other sources. There was a period when a business could thrive by publishing pages like this at scale. With AI Overviews and AI Mode directly answering most informational queries, those pages no longer have a viable purpose. The most recent update to the Quality Rater Guidelines increased the use of the word "paraphrased" from three mentions to twenty-five. Liz Reid's assessment of what gets clicked in AI Overviews is direct: surface-level AI-generated content does not get clicked because clicking it teaches the user nothing beyond what the AI Overview already told them.

The city guide case study in her December update analysis makes this concrete. Every page she reviewed had nothing on it that could not be covered by an AI Overview. Almost everything was paraphrased from elsewhere. But the site owner's writers were actually visiting the places they wrote about, and once the pages started reflecting that — original photographs, first-hand accounts of specific dishes, "must try" recommendations from real visits, video filmed by their own team — rankings improved. The diagnosis she gives for commodity content is systematic: take a page that has declined, paste it into an LLM, and ask it to tell you whether the content adds anything truly uniquely interesting and original to the body of knowledge that already exists. If the honest answer is no, you know the problem. The follow-up question — give me ten ways to draw from real-world experience to go deeper on these topics — is where the recovery starts.


## "I personally believe that from 1998 until now, those of us who create content have been giving it to Google to train AI. In return, we got human traffic and ad revenue. I think that partnership no longer exists in its traditional form."

The most structurally significant thing Marie is tracking in 2026 is the arrival of agents as primary web actors. Google has released a new "Google-Agent" user agent specifically for when AI agents using Google infrastructure browse a site. Project Mariner, Google's browser-based agent, uses this tag. The practical implication is that an entity is now reading your site that does not behave like a human — it does not click ads, it does not scroll, it does not have a session in any traditional sense. Five AI protocols are now live or in active development that every SEO should understand: MCP (Model Context Protocol, which lets agents access backend data securely), A2A (Agent-to-Agent, enabling bot-to-bot communication and transactions), UCP (Universal Commerce Protocol, which lets a machine buy a product directly from the SERPs), A2UI (Agent to User Interface, which automatically composes visual layouts), and AG-UI (Agent User Interaction, a middleware for streaming real-time AI data).

WebMCP is the piece Marie is most focused on for the near term. Standard browser agents are slow because they navigate by looking at pixels the way a human does. WebMCP lets agents use the actual functionality of a website natively — filling out lead forms perfectly, negotiating with other agents on pricing, accessing tools without simulating mouse clicks. Gemini predicted, in a client brainstorm Marie ran, that by late 2026 some websites will be using WebMCP and by 2027 it will be commonplace. Her read is that sites implementing it before competitors will have an advantage, because agents will preferentially interact with pages that expose native tool access rather than ones that require pixel-level simulation. For eCommerce specifically, UCP is mandatory reading now — Google has published a specification and Merchant Center implementation guide, and there is a waitlist for early access.


## "In reality, it is the biggest opportunity we have seen since the invention of the search engine itself."

Marie's overall frame for this moment is not anxious. The traditional partnership where creators gave Google content and received human traffic in return is over in its old form, but she does not read that as a loss. We are no longer optimizing for clicks alone. We are optimizing for direct action — frictionless commerce, automated lead generation, and agent-to-agent transactions that require none of the friction of human browsing. Sundar Pichai said in interview that 2027 will be "an important inflection point" and "a big year in which some of those shifts happen pretty profoundly." Marie's practical advice to clients is to not let social media's concern about blocking AI bots or debating whether to call the job SEO or GEO distract from the actual preparation: get familiar with WebMCP, learn UCP if any part of the business involves eCommerce, and start learning to use agentic coding tools — she personally uses Google's Antigravity, though she encourages clients to spend a week each with Claude Code and other tools before settling on one. The people who press in and build competence with these tools now are, in her view, best positioned for the transition that is already underway.
