## "The content that performs best in AI Overviews or receives citations from ChatGPT is, more often than not, the same high-quality, well-optimized content that excels in traditional Google Search."

Lily Ray is the founder of Algorythmic, an SEO and AI search consultancy launched in April 2026, and VP of SEO and AI Search at Amsive, where she provides strategic leadership for the agency's search programs. She began her SEO career in 2010 in a startup environment and spent the next sixteen years building and leading award-winning SEO departments across major agencies, working across publisher, retail, ecommerce, B2B, and CPG verticals for Fortune 500 clients. She is the industry's most-cited analyst of Google's Quality Rater Guidelines, having tracked every major update to that document since it became publicly visible and translated its implications for practitioners in real time. Her reputation rests on a specific kind of work: reading Google's official documentation, guidelines, and communications with close enough attention to surface what they actually imply about content quality, trust, and authority — and translating those implications into language that practitioners can act on. In 2025 and 2026, that work has centered almost entirely on the question of how AI-powered search changes what it means to produce content worth ranking.

The thesis Ray argues most persistently, and with the most specific evidence, is that the arrival of AI search has not invalidated SEO. It has revealed which SEO practices were always the right ones. Google processed 14 billion searches per day in 2024 — 373 times more than the estimated daily search-like prompts on ChatGPT. Google's search volume grew by more than 21 percent in 2024 compared to 2023. Research by Glenn Gabe found that AI search drove less than one percent of traffic to most websites as of June 2025, with many sites seeing numbers below 0.5 percent. Against this backdrop, Ray's concern is not that AI search will replace traditional search — it is that the hype surrounding AI search has produced a wave of poorly reasoned content strategy advice that actively leads brands away from the practices that generate durable visibility.


## "Hundreds of self-proclaimed 'GEO experts' have suddenly materialized, each professing to possess the secret key to unlocking visibility in a marketing domain that has existed for less than a couple of years."

Ray's critique of the AI search advice ecosystem is methodical and specific. She observes that many of the entities selling "GEO" or "AEO" services as replacements for SEO are themselves AI-generated — faceless brands using AI logos and AI-automated marketing campaigns to offer large promises without any verifiable track record of earning visibility in AI answers. The tactics they sell as novel are, on close inspection, rebranded versions of practices that SEO professionals have been applying for years. Conversational, long-tail keyword research maps directly onto what LLMs use in query fan-out, but SEOs have been doing this since voice search became a topic in 2015. Multi-modal content optimization — images, video, audio — was a core SEO recommendation long before AI search existed. Digital PR, brand mentions, and entity authority have always been foundational to SEO, predating Google itself in the form of PageRank. Structured data implementation has been a best practice since 2011. The "new" content optimization advice circulating in AI search guides — write clearly, answer questions directly, use headings, break content into readable passages — is what every competent on-page SEO practitioner has been doing for the better part of two decades.

The reason these recommendations look new to some audiences is that they were never universally applied. Content operations that optimized for keyword density, volume, and thin coverage while ignoring readability, specificity, and genuine user intent did not produce the AI search visibility they were promised, and many are now being told that AI SEO requires a fundamentally different approach. Ray's reading is more sobering: those operations never built content that satisfied users, and AI ranking systems — which draw directly from Google's search index through retrieval-augmented generation — are now making that failure more visible and more consequential. The content that earns citations from ChatGPT is the content that already ranks prominently in Google. Producing low-quality content at volume to chase AI search visibility will trigger negative consequences from Google's core updates, which affect visibility across all search features including AI Overviews and AI Mode.


## "Trust is the most important member of the E-E-A-T family because untrustworthy pages have low E-E-A-T no matter how Experienced, Expert, or Authoritative they may seem."

Ray's most enduring research contribution is her analysis of Google's Quality Rater Guidelines, a document Google updates multiple times per year to instruct the human evaluators who score search results and whose scores feed into algorithm training. Her December 2022 breakdown of the guidelines identified the most significant structural change to Google's quality framework in years: the addition of "Experience" to E-A-T, creating E-E-A-T — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trust. The change was not cosmetic. It encoded a specific new standard for what counts as high-quality content. A page written by someone with credentials about a topic is not the same, in Google's framework, as a page written by someone with first-hand experience of what they are describing. A product review from a person who has actually used the product carries more trust than a review that aggregates specifications from manufacturer data. A medical article co-written by a patient describing their own treatment process adds something a credential alone does not.

The placement of Trust at the center of the E-E-A-T framework — Google's phrasing was that "untrustworthy pages have low E-E-A-T no matter how Experienced, Expert, or Authoritative they may seem" — restructured how practitioners should think about the hierarchy of quality signals. It is not enough to have credentials, citations, or the appearance of authority if the page itself gives users reason to doubt. Google's updated guidelines introduced explicit guidance on conflicts of interest: a review by a manufacturer of their own product is not trustworthy, nor is a review from an influencer paid to promote the product. The guidelines also tied reputation to individual content creators, not just to domains — a change that directly affects AI-assisted content operations where multiple unnamed contributors produce content under a single brand. If the people writing the content have no verifiable professional identity, Google's quality evaluation has no anchor for assessing their credibility.


## "The idea of breaking down long pages into smaller, indexable units to better serve user intent is, in many ways, the foundation of how AI models now synthesize answers."

One of Ray's most technically substantive arguments concerns the architectural continuity between traditional Google search and AI search. She traces the concept of "passage indexing" — Google's 2020 announcement that it could index individual passages within a page independently from the page as a whole — directly to the retrieval-augmented generation architecture that AI systems like Google AI Overviews use to synthesize answers. When an AI system breaks a long document into chunks, ranks those chunks for relevance to a query, and synthesizes the most relevant passages into an answer, it is applying the same logic Google had already embedded in its passage indexing system. The difference is scale and sophistication, not fundamental approach. Cindy Krum described this dynamic as "Fraggles" — fragments and handles — in a 2019 Moz presentation: Google was already extracting and using content fragments years before generative AI became a consumer product.

This continuity has a direct implication for content production. Pages that are structured to make their individual arguments clear and navigable — with specific, answerable headings, self-contained paragraphs, and claims supported by evidence within the passage rather than requiring reading the entire document — are not just better for featured snippets. They are structurally suited to being retrieved and synthesized by AI systems. The formatting practices that improve AI search visibility are the same ones that improve passage indexing, featured snippet eligibility, and overall user experience. Ray's framework for this is consistent: the content formats that AI systems prefer are the ones that were always easier for both humans and search engines to use. Optimizing specifically for AI extraction, as opposed to optimizing for genuine clarity and usefulness, is a category error — it treats AI systems as a separate audience with different preferences, when the evidence consistently shows they are trained on the same user behavior signals that have always determined Google's quality rankings.


## "If anyone is uniquely skilled and seasoned in navigating daily evolution, it is unquestionably SEO professionals."

The practical guidance Ray offers for content teams building AI search visibility is not a set of new tactics — it is a framework for applying established SEO principles with an updated understanding of why they matter. Brand mentions on credible third-party sites, which have always been important for off-page authority, are now directly visible in AI search citation patterns. Tools like Profound can show which domains are cited most frequently across major LLMs for a given brand query. For a company like Canva, Ray's example, the domains appearing as top citations across seven major LLMs are review sites, directories, and trusted third-party references — precisely the off-page reputation sources that digital PR has always targeted. The AI visibility measurement problem is real: LLMs produce non-deterministic outputs and no single tool gives a perfectly accurate picture. But using directional tools like Profound, Peec AI, Otterly, and updated features in Semrush and Ahrefs to monitor brand perception and citation patterns across AI platforms is a legitimate extension of share-of-voice measurement, not a replacement for it.

Ray is explicit that AEO — Answer Engine Optimization — is a genuine and important service, but only as an increment to solid SEO rather than a substitute for it. AEO requires new skills: AI and machine learning literacy, NLP and semantic search, workflow automation, and tracking methodologies that go beyond impression and click data to capture brand sentiment and citation frequency across AI platforms. The practitioners who are best positioned to develop those skills are the ones who have spent years understanding why Google rewards what it rewards. The hype cycle Ray describes — voice search, mobile-first indexing, Core Web Vitals, TikTok — has produced multiple waves of "SEO is dead" predictions, none of which proved accurate. Her read of the current AI moment is the same: the fundamentals of what search systems reward have not changed. What has changed is the sophistication with which those systems can detect whether content actually delivers what it promises — and for AI-powered content teams that have been producing high volume at low quality, that sophistication is the threat worth taking seriously.
