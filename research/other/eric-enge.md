## "The most important question in search has never changed: does this result actually serve what the user needed? AI makes that question harder to fake."

Eric Enge is President of Pilot Holding and the lead co-author of The Art of SEO, a 900-plus page reference book that has been called the bible of the SEO industry since its first edition. He founded Stone Temple Consulting in 2005, built it into one of the most respected digital marketing agencies in the country, and sold it to Perficient in 2018. In 2016, Search Engine Land named him Search Marketer of the Year and US Search Awards named him Search Personality of the Year. His career-long focus has been on empirical research — designing structured experiments, analyzing data carefully, and drawing conclusions that hold up across client work rather than from individual observations. That research orientation led him to publish a detailed comparative analysis of 62 queries across ChatGPT search and Google in December 2024, the most rigorous head-to-head comparison of the two platforms published by an independent practitioner, and it is where his thinking on AI-powered content production is most fully expressed.

The December 2024 study was unusual in its methodology. Each query required approximately one hour of fact-checking and intent analysis, for a total investment that most organizations would not authorize for exploratory research. Enge scored responses across six dimensions: errors, omissions, weaknesses, whether the query intent was fully addressed, the quality of follow-up resources provided, and overall quality. He scored queries across informational, commercial, local, navigational, and content gap analysis categories, deliberately including some that favored each platform. The overall conclusion — that Google remains the stronger platform for most searches — was less important than the category-level findings, which revealed precisely where AI search tools have genuine advantages for content practitioners and where they do not.


## "Google has been collecting user intent data since 2004 and Chrome data since 2008. That's not a gap that any new AI search tool closes quickly."

The most structurally significant finding in Enge's 62-query study is the source of Google's advantage. It is not algorithmic sophistication or model quality — it is data accumulation at a scale that is effectively unreplicable. Chrome holds 67.5 percent of the global browser market share. Google Maps has an estimated 118 million users in the United States, with Waze adding another 30 million. Every query, every click, every session that ended in satisfaction or dissatisfaction across two decades of search has been used to train Google's understanding of what user intent looks like at a granular, query-by-query level. Enge points to the example of a user searching "Merrell Shoes" — what appears to be a simple navigational query — who in reality visited six websites and executed four additional queries before completing a transaction on Zappos. Google's models are trained on millions of sessions like this one, teaching them to understand that what users ask for and what they actually need are often different things. This is why Google scored 6.25 on local queries while ChatGPT search scored 2.0, and why Google scored 6.44 on commercial queries to ChatGPT's 3.81. The gap is not a product gap. It is a data gap that will take years to close.

For content creators, the practical implication of this data advantage is about how Google evaluates content rather than how AI tools perform as search interfaces. Google's ranking systems are trained on the same user behavior signals that its search product is trained on. When a page receives a click and the user returns to the SERP within seconds, that is a negative signal. When a user lands on a page and does not return — because they found what they needed — that is a positive signal. Enge's research into query session dynamics makes clear that satisfying a user often means anticipating the follow-up questions they will have, not just answering the initial query. Pages that answer one question narrowly, without providing pathways to related information the user will need, tend to lose sessions even when they are technically accurate. Content strategy that treats each page as a standalone answer, rather than as part of a user's evolving understanding of a topic, misses how Google's systems actually evaluate satisfaction.


## "ChatGPT search won on content gap analysis by a wide margin. That tells you something important about where AI tools fit in a content team's workflow."

The one category where ChatGPT search substantially outperformed Google in Enge's study was content gap analysis: 3.25 to 1.0. This finding is not incidental. Content gap analysis — providing a URL of an existing page and asking an AI to identify what is missing or could be improved — is a class of query that requires synthesizing across sources and reasoning about completeness in a way that traditional search is not designed to do. Google returns what exists. ChatGPT can identify what is absent. For content teams, this represents a genuine division of labor: AI-assisted tools are well-suited to the research and planning phase of content production, helping practitioners find the gaps that their existing content fails to address, while Google remains the better tool for understanding what the competitive landscape already covers and what searchers have validated through their behavior.

Enge's framework for applying AI tools to content creation is consistently one of augmentation rather than replacement. His study found significant errors in three out of four content gap analysis queries when using ChatGPT search — meaning the tool's outputs require expert review and cannot be published without verification. He uses the term "garbage-in, garbage-out" to describe what happens when AI tools synthesize from stale or inaccurate web sources: the model may confidently attribute information to a source that no longer supports it, or describe a person's current role incorrectly because the web contains old information. For content production, this failure mode is the one that most damages brand credibility, because it is the kind of error that a reader with domain knowledge will catch immediately, and a fact-checker reviewing their own work may miss precisely because they know the correct answer and skim past the AI's version. The editorial review step is not a quality assurance formality. It is the step that distinguishes content that builds authority from content that quietly erodes it.


## "The web is full of content that looks good at a glance and falls apart when you check. That's the problem AI makes worse, and also the problem that makes deep expertise more valuable."

Enge spent much of his Stone Temple years researching what he called the authority problem in content marketing: the fact that the web rewards the appearance of expertise more reliably than the reality of it. His work on content marketing and competitive advantage repeatedly returned to the observation that sustainable search performance comes from content that domain experts within an organization actually know and that competitors cannot easily replicate. In the AI content era, this observation has become more pointed. AI tools can produce content that closely resembles expert writing on most topics, which raises the floor of what "looks credible" while doing nothing to raise the floor of what is actually accurate. The result is more content that appears authoritative and contains errors, which makes the credibility gap between genuine expertise and simulated expertise simultaneously harder to detect and more consequential when detected.

His analysis of Google SGE (now AI Overviews) in 2023 identified a parallel pattern. The generative experience performed well on clear factual queries where the answer was unambiguous and well-sourced online. It struggled on queries that required reasoning across multiple sources, disambiguation of intent, or recognition of what the user would need to do after getting an initial answer. These are precisely the query types where experienced practitioners, subject matter experts, and writers who have actually done the thing they are writing about add value that AI systems cannot replicate from the aggregate web. The implication for content strategy is consistent across both the SGE research and the 2024 ChatGPT study: the content types that AI handles best are informational and reference content where accuracy is verifiable and the answer is stable. The content types where humans maintain a durable advantage are those requiring original observation, fresh data, professional judgment, or reasoning across ambiguity.


## "Whoever wrote the best resource on a topic a decade ago had an advantage. Whoever writes the best resource now has a bigger one, because the alternatives have multiplied and the gap is visible."

The Art of SEO, which Enge co-authored with Stephan Spencer and Jessie Stricchiola and has now gone through multiple editions, reflects a consistent philosophy: SEO is not a set of tactical hacks applied to content, but an engineering discipline applied to the full relationship between a website, its content, and the users it serves. That philosophy means that the frameworks in the book — authority, relevance, user experience, technical accessibility, and trustworthiness — are not tactics that go out of date when algorithms change. They are the underlying dimensions that search systems, whether traditional or AI-powered, are designed to measure. Enge's position on AI content production follows from this: the question is not whether to use AI tools, but whether the content produced using those tools scores well on the underlying dimensions that search systems reward.

His practical guidance for content teams combines the structural insight from his research with operational discipline. The content gap analysis capability of ChatGPT-class tools is genuinely useful for identifying where existing content is incomplete — but every gap the tool identifies needs to be verified by someone who actually knows the topic, because the tool's error rate on specifics is high enough that acting on unverified suggestions will produce content with confident-sounding mistakes. Original research, primary sources, and direct expert involvement remain the clearest signals to both human readers and AI ranking systems that a piece of content has something to offer beyond synthesis of what already exists online. Enge's view is that the practitioners who will do best in the AI search era are those who treat AI as an accelerant for their own expertise rather than a substitute for it — using it to go faster and broader on the research, planning, and draft generation phases while investing more deliberately in the original insight and expert review phases that distinguish content worth citing from content worth ignoring.
