## "There are no shortcuts to the top of Google. But there is a faster path, and it runs through content other people already want to link to."

Brian Dean founded Backlinko in 2012 and built it into one of the most cited SEO research blogs in the industry before selling it to Semrush in 2023. His method was never about tactics in isolation — it was about understanding what the data actually shows and building systems around those findings. Where most SEO advice was anecdotal, Dean ran controlled experiments and published the numbers. The blog's defining studies, including a 912 million blog post analysis co-authored with BuzzSumo and the original Skyscraper Technique case study, gave practitioners something rare in the field: replicable methodology backed by large-scale evidence. His work is directly relevant to the AI content era not because he predicted it, but because the patterns he identified — what earns attention, what earns links, what actually drives traffic versus what merely fills a content calendar — are the patterns AI search systems are now being trained to detect.

The central empirical finding in Brian Dean's work is that most content fails completely and a small fraction earns nearly everything. The 912 million post study found that 94 percent of all published content earns zero links. Not a few links — zero. The implication for anyone who uses AI to scale content volume is stark: producing more of what doesn't work does not improve outcomes, it compounds the problem. Dean's diagnosis of why most content fails is precise: it is what he calls "me-too content" — articles that paraphrase the same information that already exists at the top of Google, adding nothing that a search engine cannot already find a better version of. In the era before AI Overviews, me-too content could sometimes capture traffic by sheer volume or minor keyword variation. With AI systems now synthesizing and delivering that information directly in the SERP, the floor for what constitutes useful content has risen substantially. Content that duplicates what the AI Overview will say is invisible in the same way that commodity content has always been invisible — AI just makes the mechanism faster and more complete.


## "94% of all content gets zero links. The rest follows a power law. Your job is to be in the top 6%, and there's a specific way to do that."

The Skyscraper Technique is Brian Dean's most cited contribution, and it is worth understanding precisely because it is not a content writing formula — it is a research methodology for identifying where the bar is set before writing a word. The process has three steps: find a piece of content in your niche that has already earned substantial links, create something demonstrably better than that piece, and reach out to the people who linked to the original. In a case study he published, this approach produced an 11 percent email response rate using highly personalized outreach and a 110 percent increase in organic traffic within fourteen days. What the technique encodes is a specific way of thinking: start with what the market has already validated as link-worthy, then ask what is missing or what could be improved. That question — what does this content lack? — is precisely the question AI content gap analysis tools are now designed to answer.

The 912 million post study surfaced several findings that challenge common assumptions about what kind of content earns links versus what gets shared. Long-form content over 3,000 words earned 77.2 percent more backlinks than shorter pieces, but content length alone did not drive this — it was the comprehensiveness that length made possible. List posts attracted 218 percent more links than how-to articles, which surprised many content teams who had been told how-to guides were the most linkable format. Shares and links showed almost no correlation: content that spread widely on social media did not reliably earn editorial links, and content that earned links rarely went viral on social. This split matters enormously for AI-assisted content strategy, because most AI content optimization tools are trained on engagement signals — shares, comments, time on page — rather than the link behavior that actually drives domain authority. The study also found that original research earned approximately three times more backlinks than any other content format, which is the finding Dean himself has most frequently cited in explaining what 10x content actually means in practice.


## "If you want to rank in 2025, you need to create content that is 10 times better than anything else out there. Not 10 percent better. Ten times better."

The 10x content concept is the principle that connects Brian Dean's research findings to the practical problem of the AI era. He did not coin the term — Rand Fishkin did — but Dean operationalized it through his Backlinko case studies in a way that made it teachable. 10x content is not subjectively "better." It has specific characteristics: it is more thorough than any competing piece, it contains information that cannot be found elsewhere in one place, it has a format that makes the information genuinely easier to use, and it provides something the searcher could not have gotten from the existing top results. In the context of AI-powered content production, this definition becomes a quality gate rather than an aspiration. Running any piece of content through an LLM and asking it to evaluate whether the content adds anything that cannot be synthesized from existing sources is a direct application of the 10x test. Dean's practical version of this question: would someone save this page to reference later, or just read it and move on?

The distinction between content that earns passive attention and content that drives active engagement is where Dean's work has the most to offer anyone building an AI-assisted content operation. He found consistently that the posts generating the most long-term search traffic were not the posts his team thought would perform best at the time of writing — they were the posts that went deepest on questions people were genuinely trying to resolve. His case study on the backlinko.com/seo-techniques page, for example, was written to be a comprehensive reference rather than a timely news post, and it continued accumulating links and traffic years after publication. This compounding behavior — content that keeps earning long after the initial push — is the direct opposite of what most AI content factories produce. High-volume, low-depth content produces high-volume, low-lifetime results. The 10x posts become assets. The me-too posts become liabilities as they age and accumulate thin content signals.


## "Most people ask 'how do I make content?' The right question is 'what content does the market not have yet?'"

Brian Dean's approach to content research consistently prioritized the gap between what searchers need and what the existing content ecosystem provides. His Skyscraper Technique starts from what already earned links; his content ideation process starts from what competitors have not yet covered at depth. In the AI era, this gap-finding function is one of the places where LLMs genuinely accelerate skilled practitioners rather than replace them. Feeding a topic and a URL to an AI system and asking it to identify what the current content is missing is a natural extension of the research methodology Dean had been applying manually. The AI can surface omissions, suggest angles, flag gaps in coverage — but the judgment about which gaps matter to a real reader, and what original perspective the writer can bring to filling them, remains outside what AI tools reliably do.

What made Dean's blog itself remarkable was that it functioned as a demonstration of the principles he was teaching. The Backlinko posts were long, heavily researched, visually structured, and each covered a topic in more depth than any other page on the same subject. The result was that competitor sites routinely linked to Backlinko as a resource even when writing content that competed with it directly. This is the dynamic that 10x content creates: when your piece becomes the authoritative reference in a space, it earns links from people who disagree with you, update their own articles, or are simply trying to cite the best source. In a landscape where AI can produce hundreds of adequate pieces on any topic, the ability to produce the single definitive piece on a topic — the one that earns citations from AI Overviews, earns editorial links, and remains the reference that practitioners actually send each other — is the sustainable competitive advantage that Brian Dean spent twelve years demonstrating was achievable and describable.


## "SEO is not about gaming a system. It's about building something valuable enough that the system wants to show it."

After selling Backlinko to Semrush in 2023, Brian Dean shifted focus to entrepreneurship and startup-building, but the body of work he left behind has taken on new relevance in the AI search era precisely because it never depended on tactics that AI systems could easily circumvent. The Skyscraper Technique, the 10x content framework, and the 912 million post study were all built around what users actually value and what the link economy actually rewards — not what the algorithm happened to be measuring at a particular moment. When Google and other AI systems shifted toward training their ranking signals on user satisfaction rather than on any single SEO variable, the content philosophy Dean articulated became more aligned with what search systems reward, not less.

The practical inheritance of his work for AI-powered content production teams is a research methodology rather than a template library. Teams using AI to produce high volumes of content benefit most from Dean's framework when they apply it as a filter: before generating content at scale, identify which topics have a genuine gap between what searchers need and what the best current content provides. Produce AI-generated drafts as starting points, not endpoints. Use original data, original research, or genuine hands-on expertise to add the layer that no other piece on the topic has. Apply the 10x test before publishing: not whether the content is good, but whether it is better than everything else at the same query in a way that a knowledgeable reader would immediately recognize. The 94 percent finding is a useful discipline. Most content will earn zero links whether produced by humans or AI. The question is whether the 6 percent — the content that actually accumulates authority over time — is being built with the deliberateness that earning that position requires.
