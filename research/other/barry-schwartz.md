## "Machine learning and AI is very, very significant in terms of how Bing and Google figure out algorithm rankings in search."

Barry Schwartz is the founder of Search Engine Roundtable and news editor at Search Engine Land, and has been reporting on search engines since 2003. He started because a software client asked how to get into search engines, and Barry found the early WebmasterWorld and SEOChat forums so fascinating that he simply started logging the important conversations. What began as a hobby never stopped. His software company, RustyBrick, is where he makes his money. The blog is something he does because he loves it.

The early SEO forums — WebmasterWorld, SEOChat, High Rankings, Jim World — were where the entire community lived. Nobody used real names, everyone had aliases, and threads ran to five or six paragraphs with citations and screenshots. Google employees would occasionally participate under pseudonyms and tell people when their theories were wrong. Barry misses that depth. He sees today's TikTok and Instagram SEO advice as often completely wrong, and thinks the community bears some responsibility for not meeting newer audiences where they are.


## "The trend is clear. Google wants to rank the most relevant, most useful, most helpful pieces of content."

The biggest algorithm milestones Barry has tracked over 20 years: Florida (2002–2003) was the first major signal that spam and junk content would not be tolerated. Panda targeted low-quality content farms — massive operations like Mahalo that were generating thin content purely for rankings collapsed overnight. Penguin hit the link-building community hard, because up to that point you could rank anything for any query with enough quality links, regardless of whether your site was actually about that topic. After Penguin, the debate of "is content king or are links king" effectively ended. Content won.

The direction has never changed, only the sophistication of enforcement. Google wants to index and rank only the best of what is available, and in English there is almost nothing you can write about that has not already been written about many times. Google has enormous choice. It is only going to rank the most useful content.


## "If you really build something that users want and it's helpful, you'll get links, you'll get people going to your website, and Google will be able to understand your content is really useful."

Ranking factors lists and reverse-engineering claims were common in the early days, when Google was mostly about links. Barry rarely sees those claims anymore. The algorithm is now hundreds if not thousands of signals weighted differently depending on query type. It is nearly impossible to reverse-engineer at that level of complexity.

His practical advice is simple: use a search-engine-friendly CMS — almost every modern one qualifies — and then build content that people actually want to consume in any format, whether video, images, or written posts. Google is very good at consuming all of it. The fundamentals have not changed in 20 years. Technical SEO as a discipline was genuinely difficult in the early 2000s when every CMS generated dynamic, unparseable URLs. Today it is nearly a non-issue. That entire category of work has been solved by the platforms themselves.


## "Google doesn't really have to make publishers happy. All they really care about is making the searcher happy so they come back for more searches."

The sharpest insight Barry shares in the interview came from a private meeting with Amit Singhal, who ran search at Google for many years. Featured snippets had just launched without visible links to the source. Barry pushed back on behalf of publishers. Singhal's response stopped him cold: Google's number one priority is to make the searcher happy. Publishers are necessary, but they are not the customer. The customer is the person doing the search.

That conversation changed how Barry thinks about every Google product decision since. The question is not how any change affects publishers or SEOs. The question is whether it makes the searcher more satisfied, more likely to return, and more likely to click an ad.


## "AI content is not there yet. But I think you could leverage AI to build a great piece of content as long as a human fills in the blanks."

On AI and machine learning in search, Barry draws a clear distinction between AI used inside Google's ranking systems and AI used by SEOs to produce content. On the ranking side, machine learning is deeply embedded across featured snippets, layout decisions, and ranking signals. MUM gets a lot of attention but is actually deployed in very limited, specific applications — not the sweeping change many assumed.

On the content production side, the Helpful Content Update landed squarely on machine-generated content at scale. Barry's read is that Google identified the pattern: fast output that looks like quality content but is not. His position on AI-assisted writing is nuanced. Using AI to supplement content, fill in gaps, or surface angles a writer had not thought of is fine and potentially useful. Publishing content that is entirely machine-generated without human review and editing is not ready, and Google has now made clear it will be penalized. The tool is legitimate. The workflow of replacing the human entirely is not.


## "The perception of SEO has always been poor from outside the SEO community. That has not changed."

Barry is candid that SEO's reputation problem is not improving. The outside world still associates the field with manipulation, even though most practitioners are trying to help relevant content rank for relevant queries. The black hat minority has defined the public image of the whole profession for decades. He thinks it will not change quickly, but notes that SEO departments now exist inside the Wall Street Journal and every Fortune 500. The industry has matured, budgets are larger, and practitioners who have survived multiple major algorithm updates have developed much thicker skin than the early community. There is more stability, more legitimacy, and significantly more tolerance for change than in 2003.


## "The most rewarding part is helping people get jobs. I mention someone in an article, they share something I write about them, and it ends up helping them get hired or get a raise."

Barry's answer on what keeps him writing after 20-plus years has nothing to do with search algorithms. People walk up to him at conferences and say "you don't know this, but you helped me" — a job they got, a speaking gig they landed because he shared their work, a raise that followed from visibility he gave them. That is what keeps him going. The community has done a lot for him, he says, and he hopes he has done a little for them.

He produces two pieces of video content every week on YouTube: a weekly recap of everything that changed in SEO, and a vlog where he interviews industry personalities. He is on Twitter as @rustybrick and writes at Search Engine Roundtable and Search Engine Land.
